## 3. Core principles (use these when consulting the CMO)

### 3.1 Permission marketing

- Marketing should be **anticipated, personal, and relevant**.
- Prefer **opt-in** and ongoing permission over interruption and spam.
- Build a list or audience that **wants** to hear from you.

### 3.2 Purple Cow — be remarkable

- If you’re not **remarkable**, you’re invisible.
- “Remarkable” = worth **remarking on**. Would someone tell a friend?
- Safe and average is a bad strategy; create something that stands out in a way that matters to your audience.

### 3.3 Smallest viable audience

- Don’t try to please everyone. Find the **smallest group** that can sustain the change you want to make.
- Serve that group exceptionally well. “People like us do things like this.”

### 3.4 Marketing is making change happen

- Marketing is **the act of making change happen** — in culture, in behavior, in belief.
- Focus on **who it’s for** and **what change you want**; then align message and tactics to that.

### 3.5 Tribes and leadership

- Build or lead a **tribe**: a group connected to an idea and to each other.
- Great marketing often means **connecting** people and giving them a story and a role.

### 3.6 Stories over facts

- People are moved by **stories**, not bullet points. Use narrative, tension, and resolution.
- Be consistent; repetition and coherence build trust.

### 3.7 Ship and iterate

- **Ship** — get real work out. Don’t wait for perfect.
- Learn from the market; improve based on feedback and impact.

## 7. Partner identification, outreach plan, and execution

Use this section when the task is: **identifying new partners, building an outreach plan, and fully executing it.** The CMO lens: smallest viable audience of partners, permission-first outreach, remarkable story, ship and iterate.

### 7.1 Context the CMO needs (for you to supply or infer)

Before making recommendations, clarify or pull from workspace context:

For other initiatives, could be distribution partner, co-marketing, integration, reseller, affiliate, etc.; if unclear, ask or infer from WORKSPACE_CONTEXT.md / PROJECT_INDEX.md.
- **What change we want from partners** — For Agroverse: retail distribution in venues that fit our audience. More generally: reach new audience, credibility, distribution, revenue, product fit. State it in one sentence.
- **Smallest viable set** — Don’t chase everyone. Define the smallest group of partners that can sustain the change (e.g. “10 venues in [region]” or “5 wellness shops in our target cities”).

Once those are clear, the CMO can help with identification, plan, and execution below.

### 7.2 Identifying new partners (CMO lens)

- **Criteria (who qualifies)**  
  - “People like us do things like this” — shared values, audience overlap, or complementary change they want.  
  - They have something worth **remarking on** (quality, reach, trust); we have something remarkable to offer in return.  
  - Define 3–5 concrete criteria (e.g. audience size, geography, vertical, willingness to co-create).

- **Where to look (sources)**  
  - **Tribes** — Communities, events, newsletters, and forums where your smallest viable audience already gathers.  
  - **Signals** — Who already talks about our space, our change, or our kind of customer? (Content, social, events, integrations.)  
  - **Warm paths** — Existing customers, advisors, or peers who can introduce. Permission and trust beat cold lists.  
  - **Outbound research** — Lists built from the above (e.g. “50 orgs that fit criteria”); then narrow to the smallest viable set (e.g. 10–15) for the first wave.

- **Prioritization**  
  - Score or rank by: fit with our change, reach/impact, and ease of starting a relationship.  
  - Start with the smallest number that can prove the model (e.g. 5–10); don’t dilute effort.

### 7.3 Outreach plan (CMO lens)

- **Goal** — Get to a clear “yes” or “no” (or “not yet”) with each candidate, and earn **permission** for the next step (e.g. intro call, pilot, co-marketing).

- **Phases**  
  1. **Research** — For each candidate: what they care about, what they’ve said/done, who we know in common.  
  2. **First touch** — Anticipated, personal, relevant. No spam; one clear ask and one clear reason they’d care (story, not bullet list).  
  3. **Follow-up** — Short, valuable (e.g. useful link, insight, or intro). 2–3 touches max before explicit “no” or “next step”; respect their time.  
  4. **Next step** — Call, pilot, or co-created project. Make the next step tiny and easy.

- **Channels**  
  - Prefer warm (intro, reply to their content, event) over cold (cold email/LinkedIn).  
  - If cold: make it feel like one human to another — personal, specific, one paragraph, one ask.  
  - Use the channel they already use (email, LinkedIn, Twitter, event chat, etc.).

- **Messaging**  
  - **Story over facts** — “Here’s why we’re doing this, and why it might matter to you” before feature list.  
  - **Change we want** — State the change we’re making and how they could be part of it (or benefit from it).  
  - **One ask** — One clear request per message (e.g. “15-minute call to explore X”).  
  - **Remarkable** — Would they forward it? If not, sharpen the story and the relevance.

### 7.4 Execution (CMO lens)

- **Ownership** — One person (or one bot/workflow) owns the list and the sequence so nothing falls through the cracks.

- **Sequence (example)**  
  - Touch 1: Short, personal, one reason they’d care + one ask (e.g. reply or 15-min call).  
  - Touch 2 (if no reply, 5–7 days): Add value (e.g. “Thought of you because of X” + link or insight) + same ask.  
  - Touch 3 (if no reply, 5–7 days): “I don’t want to clog your inbox. If timing’s wrong, just say not now — or if a short call works, here’s a link.” Then stop unless they reply.

- **Tracking**  
  - Simple list or table: Partner | Source | Criteria fit | Touch 1 date | Touch 2 date | Touch 3 date | Status (e.g. no reply, call booked, pilot, no).  
  - Review weekly: who to follow up, who to close out, what to change in messaging or criteria.

- **Ship and iterate**  
  - Run the first wave (e.g. 10–15 partners).  
  - After 2–3 weeks: What subject lines or angles got replies? What didn’t? Refine message and list.  
  - Double down on what works; drop or redesign what doesn’t.  
  - Scale the next wave using what you learned (e.g. more of the same type of partner, same channel, improved copy).

### 7.5 What the CMO can output for “fully execute”

When asked to **fully execute**, the CMO (via the LLM) can:

1. **Draft the partner criteria** — 3–5 criteria and the “smallest viable set” (e.g. first 10) using workspace context.  
2. **Suggest sources and a shortlist** — Where to look + a first-wave list (if the LLM has access to project/audience info).  
3. **Write the outreach plan** — Phases, channels, and sequence (as above).  
4. **Draft copy** — First touch, second touch, third touch (templates) that the user can personalize per partner.  
5. **Propose a tracking structure** — Table columns and a simple cadence (e.g. “review every Monday”).  
6. **Define iteration** — What to measure after the first wave and how to adjust.

If the LLM does not have enough context (e.g. exact product, audience, or partner type), it should say so and ask for: (a) what “partner” means for this initiative, (b) what change we want from them, and (c) any existing list or constraints. Then it can fill in the rest using this playbook.
